Student Learning & Assessment

I use active learning activities like Think Pair Share in the classroom to engage students in the material and improve students’ understanding and retention of class material. For example, I led a lab section introducing students to new statistical software, which may have been difficult and overwhelming. However, I used demonstrations, group discussions, and scaffolded independent work (lesson plan linked here) and students were able to successfully run simple statistics by the end of the hourlong lesson and share their results with the class.

As a teacher, I prioritize making my courses fair and accessible to all students. As a first-generation college student, I understand the importance of creating a level playing field where all students have the chance to succeed. To do so, I must create a safe, supportive learning environment and make expectations clear to all students, including those from diverse backgrounds whose expectations may differ from my own. One way to level the playing field for all students is to set clear expectations, so students know precisely what is needed to succeed. To this end, I have created and graded with rubrics to ensure clear and fair grades (sample rubric linked here) and to easily give students feedback on the strengths and weaknesses of their work.

I use multiple forms of assessment to give students plenty of opportunities to demonstrate their learning (e.g., in-class activities, formal writing assignments, multiple choice exams). I include low and no-stakes opportunities for students to apply what they are learning (e.g., informal blog posts applying class material to their everyday experiences). I find that students learn more effectively when they are comfortable voicing confusion, questions, and uncertainty, so I encourage students to raise questions or point out topics that are unclear. For example, I periodically give students an “exit ticket” survey at the end of class to assess what students found clear or not during that class session. This feedback allows me to ensure that the entire class is on track with my learning objectives.

  • “My TA makes us all feel very comfortable and relaxed in class. I do not feel pressure to have to understand everything perfectly and I appreciate that.” (student mid-semester feedback, March 11, 2021)

I am devoted to using evidence-based practices in my teaching. I plan to read the current research on teaching and learning and attend workshops and conferences to hone my teaching skills further. I am particularly interested in implementing new active learning techniques and forms of student assessment.
